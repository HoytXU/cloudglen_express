A continuous random line

Let a and b be two real numbers. Let τ (Greek “tau”) be a parameter between 0 and 1 that measures progress from a to b. Define the linear interpolation operator L(a,b;τ) = (1−τ)a + τb. The interpolated value may therefore be written x(τ)=L(a,b;τ). Thus L(a,b;0)=a and L(a,b;1)=b; intermediate values lie on the line segment joining a and b. GLSL implements L as mix.

In two dimensions, τ1 measures horizontal progress and τ2 measures vertical progress.

Value noise with Perlin’s fade

The shader divides the image plane into square cells and assigns a random scalar to each corner. If adjacent values are joined by straight lines, each cell has a constant slope, but the slope usually changes abruptly at the next grid point. The visible result is a corner. The fade function replaces that constant-speed transition with one that starts and ends flat.

Perlin’s quintic fade [2] preserves the endpoints: S(0)=0 and S(1)=1. Its first and second derivatives are also zero at both ends. In geometric terms, every cell reaches a shared boundary with zero slope and zero curvature, so neighbouring cells agree in value, slope, and curvature.

A point p has global image coordinates. The operator fract discards the whole-number cell index and retains only the position inside that cell: ξ=fract(p).

The subscripts in vij identify a corner: i=0 or 1 selects left or right, and j=0 or 1 selects bottom or top. Applying S to the two local coordinates gives w=(S(ξ1),S(ξ2)). These are interpolation weights, not a second spatial position. The symbols b0 and b1 below denote the horizontal blends along the bottom and top rows. Their vertical blend is the final value N(p). The production shader reads its corner samples from a blue-noise texture [4]; the teaching heatmap uses a deterministic hash to provide the same kind of scalar corner data without requiring that texture.

The visual below samples the square region [0,4]². Each image position represents a point p=(x,y), and its grayscale brightness represents the scalar value N(p): black denotes 0 and white denotes 1. The thin grid marks the sixteen unit cells. The marked point illustrates one evaluation of the field. Grayscale is only a numerical legend here; it is not the cloud colour used by the scene.

ξ = fract(p),   w = (S(ξ1), S(ξ2))
b0 = L(v00,v10;w1)
b1 = L(v01,v11;w1)
N(p) = L(b0,b1;w2) 

vec2 f = fract(p);
vec2 u = f*f*f*(f*(f*6.0-15.0)+10.0);

return mix(mix(a, b, u.x),
           mix(c, d, u.x), u.y);

Adding detail with octaves

Let N:ℝ²→[0,1] be the value-noise field from Section 1. A single evaluation scale contains broad variation but little fine structure. Additional copies of N, evaluated at successively higher spatial frequencies and lower amplitudes, add smaller features. Write F3,0.7 for the normalized combination of three such scales; the subscript records three scales and an amplitude multiplier of 0.7.

F3,0.7(p) = [0.70N(20p) + 0.71N(21p) + 0.72N(22p)] / [0.70+0.71+0.72] = [N(p) + 0.7N(2p) + 0.49N(4p)] / 2.19.

This is the general formula with K=3 and g=0.7 substituted into it. The sum contains exactly the indices k=0,1,2: 0.7²=0.49, 2²=4, and 1+0.7+0.49=2.19. Thus the two displayed forms are the same expression, not different definitions.

Each term is called an octave. More generally, K denotes a positive integer number of octaves, and g denotes an amplitude gain strictly between 0 and 1. The index k counts the octaves from 0 to K−1, and Σ means “sum these terms over k.” The resulting normalized octave sum is commonly called fractal Brownian motion (fBm) in procedural graphics [3].

In the general expression below, multiplying p by 2k makes octave k vary 2k times as rapidly in space, while gk reduces its contribution. Division by Σgk makes the weights sum to 1, so FK,g(p) remains between 0 and 1 whenever N(p) does.

The code implements these symbols iteratively. At the beginning of iteration k, amplitude equals gk and the mutated p equals 2kp0, where p0 is the original input. sum accumulates the numerator, weight accumulates the denominator, and the final division performs the normalization.

The four heatmaps below use the same base region p∈[0,4]². The first panel is exactly the field from Section 1. Multiplying the input by 2 and 4 fits twice and four times as many variations into each spatial direction; it changes feature size, not the output range. The listed weights determine how strongly those finer patterns contribute to the lower-right normalized sum. The production clouds continue the same process for eight octaves.

FK,g(p) = ΣK−1k=0 gkN(2kp)ΣK−1k=0 gk 

float sum = 0.0, weight = 0.0;
float amplitude = 1.0;
float gain = 0.7;
for (int k = 0; k < K; ++k) {
  sum += amplitude * noise(p);
  weight += amplitude;
  p *= 2.0;
  amplitude *= gain;
}
return sum / weight;

Layers and parallax

Let x denote horizontal image position and let t denote nonnegative time. The subscript i identifies one cloud layer, and di is that layer’s positive apparent-distance parameter. Its horizontal displacement is written Δxi(t); the capital Greek letter Δ denotes a change in position. Negative displacement denotes motion to the left.

Δxi(t) = −t/di,   dΔxi/dt = −1/di

Consequently, the speed magnitude is 1/di: large di gives slow motion and small di gives fast motion. This distance-dependent motion is the parallax cue used by the scene. The derivative dΔxi/dt denotes displacement change per unit time—that is, signed apparent velocity.

The shader samples at x−Δxi(t)=x+t/di, so a left-moving feature is generated by a right-moving lookup point. To sample layer i, λi sets horizontal frequency, φi adds a phase offset, and ηi selects a fixed vertical coordinate in the noise field. The scalar qi is the resulting horizontal coordinate, while qi is the complete two-dimensional sampling position. The scalar zi is the field value sampled there. Finally, mi is mean height, Ai is vertical amplitude, and hi(x,t) denotes the final layer height. The teaching visual fixes ηi=1.7 for every layer.

The subtraction of ½ centres the field around zero. Therefore Ai determines the vertical displacement range and mi places that range at the required height. In the visual, layers with d=20,9,4,2 move at respective speeds 1/20,1/9,1/4,1/2; the smallest distance therefore moves fastest.

qi(x,t) = λi(x + t/di) + φi
qi(x,t) = (qi(x,t), ηi)
zi(x,t) = F3,0.7(qi(x,t))
hi(x,t) = mi + Ai[zi(x,t) − ½] 

float q = frequency
        * (x + time / distance)
        + phase;
return middle + spread
        * (cloudNoise(vec2(q, 1.7)) - 0.5);

Train and bridge masks

A spatial mask M assigns every point p in the two-dimensional image plane a number from 0 to 1. This mapping is written M:ℝ²→[0,1]. The values 0 and 1 represent exclusion and inclusion, respectively; intermediate values represent partial edge coverage. A binary disk of centre c and radius r>0 is defined by the Euclidean distance ‖p−c‖. The symbol 𝟙 below is an indicator: it equals 1 when the condition in its subscript is true and equals 0 otherwise.

Mdisk(p) = 𝟙{‖p−c‖ < r}

The teaching shader replaces the hard disk boundary with a transition of width ε > 0. Rectangular indicator functions form the body and chimney, while fract(k u.x) maps every horizontal interval of length 1/k to [0,1), thereby repeating carriages and bridge spans. In this code expression, u.x is normalized horizontal position and k is the positive repetition count.

The function smoothstep(r,r+ε,d) equals 0 for d≤r, equals 1 for d≥r+ε, and changes smoothly between them. Subtracting it from 1 gives an antialiased disk. The pointwise maximum of several binary masks is their union: the combined mask equals 1 whenever any component mask equals 1.

The visual displays mask values directly: black is 0 and white is 1. The production shader keeps wagon, roof, locomotive, chimney, wheel, and bridge masks separate so that their colours can be assigned later. Its small final-render wheels use a hard squared-distance test; the smoother disk below is retained only to make the mask boundary visible.

Mwheel(p) = 1 − smoothstep(
r, r + ε, ‖p−c‖)
Mtrain(p) = max(
Mbody(p), Mchimney(p),
Mwheels(p))
Mcombined(p) = max(
Mtrain(p), Mbridge(p)) 

float wheel = 1.0 - smoothstep(
  radius, radius + 0.004,
  length(uv - center)
);
float train = max(max(body, chimney), wheels);
float combined = max(train, bridge);

A widening, irregular smoke plume

Let xfrag∈ℝ² denote pixel coordinates and let H>0 denote the viewport height. The production geometry uses the height-normalized coordinate p = xfrag/H. Let pchim = 0.49 be the chimney’s horizontal coordinate. For pixels behind the chimney, define ρ = pchim−p1 ≥ 0 as horizontal distance from the source.

Let μ(p,t) denote the plume’s centre height and let δ(ρ) denote its half-width. The smoke mask MS equals 1 precisely when the pixel height p2 lies within that half-width.

MS(p,t) = 𝟙{|p2−μ(p,t)| < δ(ρ)}

The field G(p,t) denotes the moving eight-octave noise that perturbs the centre height. The following equations define G, μ, and δ explicitly. The vector a(t)=(0.16t+3.5,0) is the horizontal noise translation, and e is the base of the natural exponential function.

The field G is F8,0.9 translated horizontally with time and shifted by 0.55. The term −0.4G perturbs the nominal centre curve 0.16√ρ+0.12. Because the square-root slope decreases as ρ grows, the centre rises rapidly near the chimney and more gradually farther away.

The half-width δ(ρ)=0.8ρe−10ρ is zero at the source, reaches its maximum at ρ=0.1, and then decreases. In the visual, the gray region is the smoke mask, the dark centre curve satisfies p2=μ(p,t), and the two boundaries satisfy p2=μ(p,t)±δ(ρ).

6

Final expression

For viewport width W>0 and height H>0, define normalized display coordinates by u=(xfrag,1/W, xfrag,2/H)∈[0,1]². Unlike the height-normalized coordinate p, this definition scales each coordinate by the corresponding viewport dimension.

For colours a,b ∈[0,1]³ and mask value m∈[0,1], define the compositing operator Blend by component-wise linear interpolation.

Blend(a,b;m) = (1−m)a + mb

Sequential application of Blend is order-dependent: a later layer replaces an earlier colour wherever its mask equals 1. The production order follows the source shader [1].

The colour vectors cbg, cT, cS, and cB denote the background, train, smoke, and bridge colours. Their masks are MT, MS, and MB. The vector cfg and scalar αfg are the foreground colour and opacity. The function V, defined below, is the vignette factor. The vectors c0 through c3 are intermediate colours after successive compositing steps.

Every colour and mask in the recurrence below is evaluated at the same pixel and time; those arguments are omitted to keep the composition legible. Train is placed over the background, smoke over the train, bridge over the smoke, and foreground clouds last. The order matters: a later layer replaces an earlier one wherever its mask equals 1.

After compositing, V:[0,1]²→[0.5,1] applies the vignette. It equals 1 at u=(½,½) and 0.5 on every image boundary, so the centre is unchanged while the edges are multiplied by one half.

c0 = cbg
c1 = Blend(c0,cT;MT)
c2 = Blend(c1,cS;MS)
c3 = Blend(c2,cB;MB)
cscene = Blend(c3,cfg;αfg)
cout(u,t) = V(u)cscene(u,t)

V(u) = ½ + ½[16u1u2(1−u1)(1−u2)]0.2
