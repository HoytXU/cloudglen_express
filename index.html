1

A continuous random line

Let a and b be two real numbers. Let τ (Greek “tau”) be a parameter between 0 and 1 that measures progress from a to b. Define the linear interpolation operator L(a,b;τ) = (1−τ)a + τb. The interpolated value may therefore be written x(τ)=L(a,b;τ). Thus L(a,b;0)=a and L(a,b;1)=b; intermediate values lie on the line segment joining a and b. GLSL implements L as mix.

In two dimensions, τ1 measures horizontal progress and τ2 measures vertical progress.

Value noise with Perlin’s fade

The shader divides the image plane into square cells and assigns a random scalar to each corner. Ordinary bilinear interpolation would leave changes in slope at cell boundaries, so the shader reshapes each local coordinate with Perlin’s quintic fade [2]. Its flat ends make neighbouring cells meet smoothly. This is value noise using Perlin’s fade—not Perlin’s gradient-noise algorithm.

Let p=(p1,p2) be a point in the image plane. The operator fract removes the integer part of each coordinate, so ξ=(ξ1,ξ2) is the point’s position inside its current grid cell. The four numbers v00,v10,v01,v11 are the values at that cell’s corners. The function S smooths each local coordinate, producing the weight vector w. In the polynomial below, r represents either local coordinate. The resulting interpolated field is denoted N(p).

ξ = fract(p),   w = (S(ξ1), S(ξ2))
S(r) = 6r5 − 15r4 + 10r3
N(p) = L(L(v00,v10;w1), L(v01,v11;w1);w2) 

vec2 f = fract(p);
vec2 u = f*f*f*(f*(f*6.0-15.0)+10.0);

return mix(mix(a, b, u.x),
           mix(c, d, u.x), u.y);

The two inner applications of L interpolate along the first coordinate axis; the outer application interpolates along the second. If v00,…,v11 are interpreted as corner elevations, N(p) is the interpolated elevation at p.

The notation S:[0,1]→[0,1] means that S accepts a number from 0 to 1 and returns a number in the same interval. It is monotone, satisfies S(0)=0 and S(1)=1, and has zero first and second derivatives at both endpoints. Thus the joined field is C²: its value, slope, and curvature are continuous across cell boundaries.

The field N:ℝ²→[0,1] is deterministic and spatially smooth: nearby positions produce nearby values, whereas distant positions vary irregularly.

The final scalar is a smooth surface value between the four random corners. The graph shows one horizontal slice of that surface; the scene uses it as the height of a cloud edge.

The corner values come from the shader’s blue-noise texture; see [4].

2

One cloud boundary

Let N:ℝ²→[0,1] be the value-noise field from Section 1. A single evaluation scale contains broad variation but little fine structure. Additional copies of N, evaluated at successively higher spatial frequencies and lower amplitudes, add smaller features. Write F3,0.7 for the normalized combination of three such scales; the subscript records three scales and an amplitude multiplier of 0.7.

F3,0.7(p) = [N(p) + 0.7N(2p) + 0.49N(4p)] / 2.19

Each term is called an octave. More generally, K denotes a positive integer number of octaves, and g denotes an amplitude gain strictly between 0 and 1. The index k counts the octaves from 0 to K−1, and Σ means “sum these terms over k.” The resulting normalized octave sum is commonly called fractal Brownian motion (fBm) in procedural graphics [3].

FK,g(p) = ΣK−1k=0 gkN(2kp)ΣK−1k=0 gk 

float sum = 0.0, weight = 0.0;
float amplitude = 1.0;
for (int k = 0; k < K; ++k) {
  sum += amplitude * noise(p);
  weight += amplitude;
  p *= 2.0;
  amplitude *= 0.7;
}
return sum / weight;

The index k identifies an octave. Multiplication of the input by 2k doubles frequency at each step, while the coefficient gk reduces its amplitude. Division by Σgk normalizes the weights to sum to 1. Hence FK,g:ℝ²→[0,1].

For the displayed comparison, K = 3 and g = 0.7. Thus the three weights are 1, 0.7, and 0.49, whose sum is 2.19.

The inputs 2p and 4p produce features one half and one quarter as wide as those in N(p).

The final clouds use F8,0.7. The smoke uses F8,0.9; its larger gain preserves more high-frequency variation.

3

Layers and parallax

Let x denote horizontal image position and let t denote nonnegative time. The subscript i identifies one cloud layer, and di is that layer’s positive apparent-distance parameter. Its horizontal displacement is written Δxi(t); the capital Greek letter Δ denotes a change in position.

Δxi(t) = t/di,   dΔxi/dt = 1/di

Consequently, large di gives low apparent velocity and small di gives high apparent velocity. This distance-dependent motion is the parallax cue used by the scene. The derivative dΔxi/dt denotes displacement change per unit time—that is, apparent velocity.

To sample layer i, λi sets horizontal frequency, φi adds a phase offset, and ηi selects a fixed vertical coordinate in the noise field. The scalar qi is the resulting horizontal coordinate, while qi is the complete two-dimensional sampling position. Finally, mi is mean height, Ai is vertical amplitude, and hi(x,t) denotes the final layer height.

qi(x,t) = λi(x + t/di) + φi
qi(x,t) = (qi(x,t), ηi)
hi(x,t) = mi + Ai [F3,0.7(qi(x,t)) − ½] 

float q = frequency
        * (x + time / distance)
        + phase;
return middle + spread
        * (cloudNoise(vec2(q, 1.7)) - 0.5);

The function hi(x,t) returns the height of layer i at horizontal position x and time t. Read the construction from the inside outward: move the sample, scale and offset it, evaluate the noise, then scale and translate the resulting height.

The teaching shader fixes ηi = 1.7, so each layer samples a horizontal slice of the 2D field. The production shader instead permits the second coordinate to vary with the pixel.

Since 0≤F3,0.7(qi)≤1, subtraction of ½ centres its output around zero. Multiplication by Ai sets the displacement range, and addition of mi sets the vertical position.

For example, d1 = 20 and d2 = 2 imply velocities 1/20 and 1/2; therefore layer 2 moves ten times as fast as layer 1.

4

Train and bridge masks

A spatial mask M assigns every point p in the two-dimensional image plane a number from 0 to 1. This mapping is written M:ℝ²→[0,1]. The values 0 and 1 represent exclusion and inclusion, respectively; intermediate values represent partial edge coverage. A binary disk of centre c and radius r>0 is defined by the Euclidean distance ‖p−c‖. The symbol 𝟙 below is an indicator: it equals 1 when the condition in its subscript is true and equals 0 otherwise.

Mdisk(p) = 𝟙{‖p−c‖ < r}

The teaching shader replaces the hard disk boundary with a transition of width ε > 0. Rectangular indicator functions form the body and chimney, while fract(k u.x) maps every horizontal interval of length 1/k to [0,1), thereby repeating carriages and bridge spans. In this code expression, u.x is normalized horizontal position and k is the positive repetition count.

Mwheel(p) = 1 − smoothstep(r, r + ε, ‖p−c‖)
Mtrain = max(Mbody, Mchimney, Mwheels) 

float wheel = 1.0 - smoothstep(
  radius, radius + 0.004,
  length(uv - center)
);
float train = max(max(body, chimney), wheels);

The GLSL function smoothstep(r,r+ε,d) equals 0 for d ≤ r, equals 1 for d ≥ r+ε, and varies smoothly between them. Subtraction from 1 therefore gives an antialiased disk mask. Pointwise max forms the union of the component masks.

The teaching figure uses ε = 0.004. The production shader instead tests squared distance with step, avoiding a square root and producing a hard boundary at its much smaller wheel scale.

If M1 and M2 are binary masks, then max(M1,M2) = 1 exactly when at least one mask equals 1. This is the set-theoretic union.

The teaching figure assigns one colour to Mtrain; the production shader keeps wagon, roof, locomotive, chimney, and wheel masks separate so each may select a different colour.

5

A widening, irregular smoke plume

Let xfrag∈ℝ² denote pixel coordinates and let H>0 denote the viewport height. The production geometry uses the height-normalized coordinate p = xfrag/H. Let pchim = 0.49 be the chimney’s horizontal coordinate. For pixels behind the chimney, define ρ = pchim−p1 ≥ 0 as horizontal distance from the source.

Let μ(p,t) denote the plume’s centre height and let δ(ρ) denote its half-width. The smoke mask MS equals 1 precisely when the pixel height p2 lies within that half-width.

MS(p,t) = 𝟙{|p2−μ(p,t)| < δ(ρ)}

The field G(p,t) denotes the moving eight-octave noise that perturbs the centre height. The following equations define G, μ, and δ explicitly; e is the base of the natural exponential function.

ρ = pchim − p1
G(p,t) = F8,0.9(p + (0.16t+3.5,0)) − 0.55
μ(p,t) = 0.16√ρ + 0.12 − 0.4G(p,t)
δ(ρ) = 0.8ρe−10ρ
MS(p,t) = 𝟙{|p2−μ(p,t)| < δ(ρ)}

The field G is F8,0.9 translated horizontally with time and shifted by 0.55. The term −0.4G perturbs the nominal centre curve. Since G depends on both coordinates of p, the plume boundary is implicit rather than the graph of a one-dimensional function.

The deterministic term 0.16√ρ+0.12 has derivative 0.08/√ρ for ρ>0; hence the nominal centre curve rises rapidly near the chimney and more gradually farther away.

For δ(ρ)=0.8ρe−10ρ, differentiation gives δ′(ρ)=0.8e−10ρ(1−10ρ). Therefore the half-width increases on [0,0.1], reaches its maximum at ρ=0.1, and decreases thereafter.

6

Final expression

For viewport width W>0 and height H>0, define normalized display coordinates by u=(xfrag,1/W, xfrag,2/H)∈[0,1]². Unlike the height-normalized coordinate p, this definition scales each coordinate by the corresponding viewport dimension.

For colours a,b ∈[0,1]³ and mask value m∈[0,1], define the compositing operator Blend by component-wise linear interpolation.

Blend(a,b;m) = (1−m)a + mb

Sequential application of Blend is order-dependent: a later layer replaces an earlier colour wherever its mask equals 1. The production order follows the source shader [1].

The colour vectors cbg, cT, cS, and cB denote the background, train, smoke, and bridge colours. Their masks are MT, MS, and MB. The vector cfg and scalar αfg are the foreground colour and opacity. The function V, defined below, is the vignette factor. The vectors c0 through c3 are intermediate colours after successive compositing steps.

c0 = cbg
c1 = Blend(c0,cT;MT)
c2 = Blend(c1,cS;MS)
c3 = Blend(c2,cB;MB)
cscene = Blend(c3,cfg;αfg)
cout(u,t) = V(u)cscene(u,t)

V(u) = ½ + ½[16u1u2(1−u1)(1−u2)]0.2

All masks and colours in the recurrence are evaluated at the same pixel and time; these arguments are suppressed to keep the composition legible.

The first pass computes cscene. Because bridge compositing follows smoke compositing, the bridge replaces smoke where both masks are nonzero; foreground clouds similarly replace all preceding layers.

The second pass applies V:[0,1]²→[0.5,1]. At u=(½,½), V=1. On any image boundary, one coordinate factor vanishes and V=0.5.
